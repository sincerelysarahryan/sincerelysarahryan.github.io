Fieldwork
After completing field training at Paradise Creek Nature Park in the Elizabeth River watershed, I joined Chesapeake Water Watch, a participatory science project whose volunteer data contributes to peer-reviewed publications. NYC Mayor's Office of Environmental Remediation
Legal & Policy Intern / Fellow
New York, NY
Policy & Law
At OER I supported applicants enrolling in the Voluntary Cleanup Program, the nation's only full service municipally-run land cleanup program, which awards green property certifications and issues grants for environmental investigation and clean-up. Reviewing remediation plans with contractors to ensure compliance gave me hands-on experience with how past land use and current zoning shape the future potential of a site. I was independently assigned to draft the foundational legal memo addressing siting, zoning, permitting, and operating requirements for what became the Clean Soil Bank, a municipal soil exchange that has since transferred over 600,000 tons of clean native soil across New York City.

Gotham Whale
Director of Advocacy
New York, NY
Advocacy
As Director of Advocacy at Gotham Whale, a volunteer organization studying the marine mammals of New York City's urban waters, I served as the bridge between our researchers and the policy world. Day to day I worked closely with researchers to learn what they had observed on the water and translated those findings into educational content and updates for our NGO coalition partners. As Gotham Whale's local voice within national coalitions, I traveled to Capitol Hill during Ocean Week to celebrate the 50th anniversary of the Marine Mammal Protection Act and educated legislative staffers on marine mammal issues specific to the New York Bight. I submitted public comments, signed onto coalition letters, and spoke at hearings in support of the designation of Hudson Canyon as a National Marine Sanctuary and reductions in menhaden catch limits. I also created the "Hey, We're Swimmin' Here" campaign raising public awareness about vessel strikes and their impact on marine mammals, including the critically endangered North Atlantic right whale.

Catch a Spat
Volunteer Scientist — Chesapeake Bay Foundation & ERP
Norfolk, VA
Restoration
Catch a Spat is an Elizabeth River Project program dedicated to improving oyster productivity in the Elizabeth River. After a training session, I deployed the cage off a dock in Freemason Harbour in downtown Norfolk, checking recycled oyster shells monthly for newly attached larvae and submitting recruitment data to the Elizabeth River Project. I joined because I wanted to contribute real monitoring data to local restoration efforts and spend more time learning the smaller species living in the river up close. I quickly learned that Freemason Harbour has an abundance of blue crabs that appreciated a free buffet. Wonderful news for the river, less wonderful for my spat count. The cage was visible from the Elizabeth River Trail, which became a natural conversation starter with neighbors and passing trail users curious about what I was doing — a small but genuine opportunity to connect the community to local conservation efforts. Successful recruits were ultimately added to the 52nd Street Reef as part of ongoing habitat restoration in the Elizabeth River watershed.

Education
Prior to law school, I served as an educator in the flagship Sail Nauticus afterschool program, teaching middle schoolers sailing, STEM, and environmental quality on the Elizabeth River and in the classroom. Topics included navigation, seamanship, weather, and communication alongside environmental quality topics such as stormwater runoff, pollution, marine debris, tides, and ecology, all grounded in the river we were sailing on. We regularly called in navigational threats to the Army Corps of Engineers and sailed with our students to dock at USACE for lessons on oyster restoration and waterway management. I filled many marketing, administrative, and coordination roles at Sail Nauticus, stepping up to keep community sailing programs running during a leadership transition.

Earth Law Center
Legal Intern & Fellow
Remote / National
Law & Policy
My most substantial contribution at Earth Law Center was a series of case studies on contaminated groundwater at schools on Long Island, which became the foundation for the documentary "Hot Water on Long Island," in which I was interviewed on camera. I interned and was later invited back as a Fellow, working with attorneys across the US on research, drafting, and editing for public-facing reports, online content, and the organization's now-published Earth Law textbook. Topics I contributed to included contaminated land, the Lake Erie Bill of Rights, Tribal Constitutions, ecocide, and cetacean captivity. Earth Law centers the environment's right to exist and thrive as a legal framework, a philosophy that continues to inform how I approach conservation and restoration work.

As a volunteer educator aboard the Atlantic Explorer, a 65-foot Whale SENSE certified catamaran operated by the Virginia Aquarium, I engage passengers as we cruise from the Owl Creek-Rudee Inlet Watershed out to the Atlantic Ocean. Our target species is the bottlenose dolphin, and we regularly encounter a variety of birds, sea turtles, and fish along the way. I recently received a compliment that my education style was refreshingly passionate, which is exactly what I aim for. Every outing is a chance to connect people to the marine environment right outside their door.
